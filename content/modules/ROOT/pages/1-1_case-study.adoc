= Solving a Real-World Challenge: Our Case Study

Batteries play a crucial role in modern industrial automation. In automotive manufacturing and logistics facilities, battery-powered autonomous mobile robots (AMRs) have become essential for efficient operations. These robots transport components, parts, and materials throughout the factory floor 24/7. Batteries act as the heart of these autonomous machines, making them one of the most critical components. An unexpected battery failure can lead to significant performance degradation and safety risks, potentially disrupting just-in-time delivery schedules and critical production operations. This is where Artificial Intelligence (AI) becomes a powerful ally.

== Developing an AI solution for factory transportation robots

In this lab, we will take the role of a systems engineer working for an automotive parts distribution center and logistics plant. Our facility operates a fleet of 18 battery-powered autonomous mobile robots that transport pallets of automotive components between receiving docks, warehouse storage, production line staging areas, and shipping docks. The team's mission is to create an end-to-end AI solution at the edge that covers two different scenarios:

=== Battery monitoring system

Our factory robots require an application responsible for monitoring battery health during their various routes throughout the facility. This application displays telemetry data collected from onboard sensors such as voltage, temperature, charge cycles, and operational distance via a graphical dashboard that can be monitored from the plant control center. In addition, this dashboard comes with an alerting system powered by AI models that detect early signs of battery stress and predict potential failure.

To support this containerized solution on each robot, a lightweight Kubernetes platform is essential. This is where Red Hat Device Edge (MicroShift) comes into play.

=== Model re-training at the edge

To ensure the AI models remain accurate and effective in detecting anomalies, they must be regularly updated with the latest data gathered from the robots during their operational routes. However, given the large volume of telemetry data generated during the robots' daily operations (40-60 trips per shift, operating 24/7), it's impractical to continuously upload all of it over the factory network.

To overcome this challenge, the system collects new data every 10 minutes. This data is then used to retrain the AI models on a more powerful edge server at the plant. For the entire AI/ML lifecycle, we rely on Red Hat OpenShift AI (RHOAI), deployed on a Single Node OpenShift (SNO) instance. The workflow is fully automated: a scheduled pipeline triggers the retraining of the models, followed by model validation. If the updated models outperform the existing ones, they are automatically deployed back to the robots, ensuring improved performance and smarter battery monitoring during plant operations.
